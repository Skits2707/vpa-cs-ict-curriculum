Week 1: Boolean Algebra — Laws and Theorems

Week 1: Boolean Algebra Foundations

Learning Objectives

  • Recall and apply the fundamental laws of Boolean algebra (identity, null, idempotent, inverse, commutative, associative, distributive, absorption)
  • Apply De Morgan's laws to simplify expressions involving complements of sums and products
  • Simplify Boolean expressions using algebraic manipulation
  • Prove equivalence of two Boolean expressions using truth tables and algebraic methods

Lesson Activities

Lesson 1 (2 hours):

  • Starter (10 min): Quick-fire truth table completion for AND, OR, NOT gates. Students use mini-whiteboards.
  • Direct Instruction (25 min): Teacher presents each Boolean law with a numerical analogy (e.g., distributive law: 3 × (4 + 5) = 3×4 + 3×5). Each law demonstrated with a truth table proof.
  • Worked Example (15 min): Simplify A · (A + B) using absorption law. Verify with truth table.
  • Guided Practice (25 min): Pairs complete scaffolded worksheet with 8 expressions to simplify. Bronze: 2-variable; Silver: 3-variable; Gold: expressions requiring multiple laws.
  • Plenary (5 min): Exit ticket: Simplify X + X' · Y without assistance.

Lesson 2 (2 hours):

  • Starter (10 min): Recall quiz on laws from previous lesson.
  • Direct Instruction (20 min): De Morgan's laws introduced with Venn diagram visualisation. Emphasise "break the bar, change the sign" mnemonic.
  • Worked Example (15 min): Simplify (A + B + C)' step-by-step using De Morgan's.
  • Guided Practice (30 min): Students work in groups of 4. Each group assigned one complex expression to simplify, then present solution to class using visualiser.
  • Independent Practice (35 min): Past paper question set (A-Level Paper 1, Q3–5 from 2018–2023). Students select bronze (3 questions), silver (5 questions), or gold (7 questions + extension proof).
  • Plenary (10 min): Class discussion: "Why is Boolean algebra essential for computer hardware design?"

Resources

  • Worksheet: Boolean Algebra Laws — Scaffolded Practice (PDF)
  • Interactive truth table generator: https://web.stanford.edu/class/cs103/tools/truth-table-tool/
  • Physical logic gate kits for demonstration (if available)
  • Past paper compilation: Boolean simplification questions 2018–2023

Assessment Points

  • Formative: Exit ticket (Lesson 1) — teacher reviews before Lesson 2
  • Formative: Group presentation rubric (Lesson 2) — peer and teacher assessment
  • Homework: Simplify 5 expressions; prove equivalence using both truth table and algebraic methods
Bronze: Simplify expressions using single laws; complete truth tables with guidance.
Silver: Apply multiple laws in sequence; verify results independently.
Gold: Prove complex equivalences; derive simplified forms from word problems; explain why particular laws apply.

Week 2: Karnaugh Maps

Week 2: Karnaugh Maps (K-Maps)

Learning Objectives

  • Construct 3-variable and 4-variable Karnaugh maps from truth tables or Boolean expressions
  • Identify and group adjacent cells following grouping rules (groups of 1, 2, 4, 8, 16)
  • Derive minimal Sum-of-Products (SOP) and Product-of-Sums (POS) expressions
  • Recognise and handle "don't care" conditions in K-map simplification
  • Compare K-map method with algebraic simplification for efficiency and accuracy

Lesson Activities

Lesson 1 (2 hours):

  • Starter (10 min): Review simplified expression from Week 1 exit ticket. Ask: "Could we find this answer without algebra?"
  • Direct Instruction (30 min): Introduce K-map grid structure. Explain Gray code ordering (00, 01, 11, 10). Show why adjacency matters. Demonstrate 3-variable K-map construction from truth table.
  • Worked Example (20 min): Teacher completes a 3-variable K-map step-by-step: populate cells, identify groups, derive minimal expression. Show both SOP and POS approaches.
  • Guided Practice (30 min): Students receive K-map templates and work in pairs on 3 problems. Teacher circulates with targeted questions: "Why did you group those cells?" "Is there a larger group possible?"
  • Plenary (10 min): Whole-class check of solutions. Common errors addressed (wrapping around edges, diagonal grouping).

Lesson 2 (2 hours):

  • Starter (10 min): Quick K-map completion from expression: F(A,B,C) = Σ(0,2,4,6)
  • Direct Instruction (20 min): 4-variable K-maps introduced. Emphasise wrap-around grouping (top-bottom, left-right, corners). Show "don't care" notation (X) and how it expands grouping possibilities.
  • Worked Example (15 min): 4-variable K-map with don't cares. Teacher thinks aloud: "This X lets me make a group of 8 instead of two groups of 4."
  • Guided Practice (35 min): Carousel activity — 6 stations around room, each with a different K-map problem. Students rotate every 6 minutes, building speed and confidence.
  • Independent Practice (30 min): Bronze: 3 three-variable problems; Silver: 2 four-variable problems; Gold: 1 four-variable with don't cares + design challenge: "A security system has 4 sensors. Use a K-map to minimise the alarm logic."
  • Plenary (10 min): Student reflection: "When would I prefer K-maps over algebra? When would algebra be faster?"

Resources

  • Printed K-map templates (3-variable and 4-variable) — one per student
  • Online K-map solver for verification: https://www.charlie-coleman.com/kmaps/
  • Logisim circuit files for verifying simplified expressions
  • Video: "Karnaugh Maps — Introduction" (Neso Academy, 15 min)

Assessment Points

  • Formative: Carousel station answers checked by teacher during rotation
  • Formative: Exit ticket — 4-variable K-map with one don't care
  • Homework: Complete 5 K-map problems; verify one using Logisim
Bronze: Complete 3-variable K-maps with teacher guidance; identify obvious groups.
Silver: Complete 4-variable K-maps independently; handle simple don't care conditions.
Gold: Solve complex 4-variable problems with multiple don't cares; design K-map from word problem; explain grouping choices rigorously.

Week 3: Floating-Point Representation

Week 3: Floating-Point Representation

Learning Objectives

  • Explain the structure of floating-point numbers: sign bit, exponent, mantissa (significand)
  • Convert denary numbers to IEEE 754 single-precision binary representation
  • Convert IEEE 754 single-precision binary back to denary
  • Explain the trade-off between precision and range in floating-point representation
  • Identify special values: zero, infinity, NaN (Not a Number)

Lesson Activities

Lesson 1 (2 hours):

  • Starter (10 min): "How would you store 0.1 in binary?" Discussion of the limitations of fixed-point representation.
  • Direct Instruction (30 min): Introduce scientific notation as analogy (e.g., 6.022 × 10²³). Extend to binary: 1.101 × 2³. Break down IEEE 754 single-precision format: 1 sign bit, 8 exponent bits (with bias 127), 23 mantissa bits. Explain hidden bit convention.
  • Worked Example (25 min): Convert +6.625 to IEEE 754 single-precision. Step-by-step: convert integer part, convert fractional part, normalise, determine exponent with bias, assemble bits.
  • Guided Practice (25 min): Pairs work on converting +9.25 and -3.5. Teacher provides conversion checklist: (1) Integer to binary, (2) Fraction to binary, (3) Normalise, (4) Apply bias, (5) Assemble.
  • Plenary (10 min): Whole-class verification using online converter. Discuss any discrepancies.

Lesson 2 (2 hours):

  • Starter (10 min): Reverse conversion starter: given binary pattern, what is the denary value?
  • Direct Instruction (20 min): Reverse conversion process: extract sign, exponent (subtract bias), mantissa (add hidden bit), calculate value. Special cases: exponent all zeros (denormalised), all ones (infinity/NaN).
  • Worked Example (15 min): Convert IEEE 754 pattern 0x40400000 to denary. Teacher demonstrates hex-to-binary breakdown.
  • Guided Practice (30 min): Students complete reverse conversion worksheet with 6 problems. Bronze: positive numbers only; Silver: include negative numbers; Gold: include special cases (infinity, zero).
  • Independent Practice (30 min): Past paper questions on floating-point from 2019–2023. Students self-select difficulty level.
  • Plenary (15 min): Discussion: "Why can't computers store 0.1 exactly? What are the implications for financial software?"

Resources

  • IEEE 754 conversion worksheets (printed)
  • Online converter for verification: https://www.h-schmidt.net/FloatConverter/IEEE754.html
  • Python demonstration: struct.pack('!f', 0.1) to show binary representation
  • Video: "How floating-point works" (Computerphile)

Assessment Points

  • Summative: Quiz 1 (Week 3) — Boolean & Floating-Point (20 marks)
  • Formative: Reverse conversion worksheet marked and returned next lesson
  • Homework: Convert 5 denary numbers to IEEE 754; explain why 0.1 + 0.2 ≠ 0.3 in floating-point
Bronze: Convert simple positive denary numbers to floating-point with structured worksheet.
Silver: Convert positive and negative numbers; perform reverse conversion accurately.
Gold: Handle special cases; explain precision limitations; discuss real-world implications (financial computing, scientific simulation).

Week 4: Normalisation & Errors

Week 4: Normalisation & Truncation Errors

Learning Objectives

  • Normalise floating-point numbers to maximise precision
  • Calculate absolute and relative error in floating-point representation
  • Explain truncation error and rounding error with examples
  • Discuss underflow and overflow conditions
  • Evaluate the suitability of floating-point for different applications

Lesson Activities

Lesson 1 (2 hours):

  • Starter (10 min): Compare two representations of the same number: 0.011 × 2⁴ vs 1.1 × 2². Which is better? Why?
  • Direct Instruction (25 min): Define normalisation: adjusting mantissa and exponent so the mantissa starts with 1 (for positive) or 0.1 (for signed). Show how normalisation maximises significant digits. Demonstrate normalising both positive and negative numbers.
  • Worked Example (15 min): Normalise 0.001101 × 2⁵ → 1.101 × 2². Show the effect on precision.
  • Guided Practice (30 min): Students normalise 8 numbers (mix of positive, negative, already normalised, and denormalised). Checklist approach: "Is the mantissa in the correct range? Did I adjust the exponent correctly?"
  • Plenary (10 min): Exit ticket: Normalise 0.000111 × 2³ and explain why this form is preferred.

Lesson 2 (2 hours):

  • Starter (10 min): Recall normalisation from previous lesson. Quick practice: is 1.01 × 2³ normalised? Is 0.11 × 2³?
  • Direct Instruction (25 min): Types of error: absolute error = |exact − approximate|; relative error = absolute error / |exact|. Truncation error: discarding less significant bits. Rounding error: adjusting last kept bit. Work through numerical examples.
  • Worked Example (15 min): Represent 1/3 in 4-bit mantissa. Calculate absolute and relative error. Discuss how error accumulates in iterative calculations.
  • Guided Practice (25 min): Problem set: calculate errors for given representations. Bronze: absolute error only; Silver: absolute and relative; Gold: error propagation in multi-step calculation.
  • Independent Practice (25 min): Research task: "Find a real-world example where floating-point error caused a significant problem." (e.g., Ariane 5, Patriot missile, Vancouver stock exchange). Students prepare 2-minute presentation.
  • Plenary (10 min): Mini-presentations (3 students). Class discussion: how could these errors have been prevented?

Resources

  • Normalisation practice worksheets
  • Error calculation problem sets
  • Case study articles: Ariane 5 explosion, Patriot missile failure
  • Python script demonstrating floating-point accumulation errors

Assessment Points

  • Formative: Exit ticket (Lesson 1) — normalisation
  • Formative: Error calculation worksheet (Lesson 2)
  • Homework: Normalise 6 numbers; calculate absolute and relative error for 4 representations; research one real-world floating-point error case
Bronze: Normalise simple positive numbers; calculate absolute error with guidance.
Silver: Normalise positive and negative numbers; calculate both absolute and relative error; explain underflow/overflow.
Gold: Handle complex normalisation; analyse error propagation; evaluate floating-point suitability for specific applications; present real-world case study.

Week 5: TCP/IP Protocol Stack

Week 5: TCP/IP Protocol Stack

Learning Objectives

  • Describe the four layers of the TCP/IP model: Application, Transport, Internet, Link
  • Explain the function of each layer and the protocols associated with it
  • Describe the encapsulation process as data moves down the stack
  • Compare TCP/IP with the OSI seven-layer model
  • Explain the role of ports in the transport layer

Lesson Activities

Lesson 1 (2 hours):

  • Starter (10 min): "How does an email get from your phone to a server in another country?" Brainstorm on whiteboard.
  • Direct Instruction (30 min): Present TCP/IP stack as a layered cake or postal system analogy. Each layer: Application (HTTP, FTP, SMTP, DNS), Transport (TCP, UDP), Internet (IP, ICMP), Link (Ethernet, Wi-Fi). Explain how each layer adds/removes headers.
  • Worked Example (15 min): Trace a web request through all four layers. Show headers added at each stage.
  • Guided Practice (25 min): Students complete a blank TCP/IP stack diagram, filling in layer names, protocols, and header information. Pairs check each other's work.
  • Plenary (10 min): Quick quiz: "Which layer handles IP addresses? Which handles reliability?"

Lesson 2 (2 hours):

  • Starter (10 min): Compare TCP/IP 4-layer model with OSI 7-layer model. Which layers correspond?
  • Direct Instruction (20 min): Deep dive into Transport layer: TCP vs UDP. TCP: connection-oriented, reliable, ordered, flow control, congestion control. UDP: connectionless, fast, no guarantees. Use cases for each.
  • Worked Example (15 min): Three-way handshake (SYN, SYN-ACK, ACK) diagrammed and explained. Sequence numbers and acknowledgements.
  • Guided Practice (25 min): Scenario cards: "Live video streaming" — TCP or UDP? Why? "Bank transaction" — TCP or UDP? Why? Students justify choices in pairs.
  • Independent Practice (30 min): Research and create a comparison table: TCP vs UDP across 8 criteria (connection, reliability, ordering, speed, overhead, use cases, header size, examples).
  • Plenary (10 min): Share comparison tables. Class vote on most surprising difference.

Resources

  • TCP/IP stack diagram posters for classroom walls
  • Wireshark packet capture demonstrations (sanitised)
  • OSI vs TCP/IP comparison handout
  • Video: "How the Internet Works in 5 Minutes" (Code.org)

Assessment Points

  • Formative: TCP/UDP scenario card justifications
  • Formative: Comparison table quality check
  • Homework: Draw complete encapsulation diagram for a DNS query; label all headers
Bronze: Name the four layers and one protocol per layer; identify TCP vs UDP for given scenarios.
Silver: Explain encapsulation process; compare TCP and UDP across multiple criteria; map TCP/IP to OSI.
Gold: Analyse packet capture data; explain three-way handshake in detail; evaluate when UDP might be preferable despite lack of reliability.

Week 6: Routing Algorithms

Week 6: Routing Algorithms

Learning Objectives

  • Explain the purpose of routing in packet-switched networks
  • Describe distance-vector routing (RIP) and its limitations (count-to-infinity)
  • Describe link-state routing (OSPF) and its advantages
  • Trace Dijkstra's shortest-path algorithm on a network graph
  • Compare static and dynamic routing approaches

Lesson Activities

Lesson 1 (2 hours):

  • Starter (10 min): "What's the shortest route from school to the city centre?" Map discussion introduces graph concepts.
  • Direct Instruction (25 min): Routing as finding optimal paths in a graph. Introduce distance-vector: each router shares its entire routing table with neighbours. Bellman-Ford equation. Demonstrate with 4-router network.
  • Worked Example (20 min): Teacher traces routing table updates across 3 iterations. Show how count-to-infinity problem arises when a link fails.
  • Guided Practice (25 min): Students given a 5-router network. Calculate initial routing tables, then update after one link failure. Identify the count-to-infinity problem.
  • Plenary (10 min): Solutions reviewed. Split-ring horizon and poison reverse introduced as solutions.

Lesson 2 (2 hours):

  • Starter (10 min): Review distance-vector limitations. "Is there a better way?"
  • Direct Instruction (25 min): Link-state routing: each router knows entire network topology. Dijkstra's algorithm introduced step-by-step. Priority queue, tentative/permanent labels.
  • Worked Example (25 min): Teacher traces Dijkstra's algorithm on a 6-node network. Table format: node, shortest distance, previous node. Students follow on identical worksheet.
  • Guided Practice (25 min): Students apply Dijkstra's to a new 7-node network. Bronze: first 3 steps provided; Silver: first step provided; Gold: complete from scratch.
  • Independent Practice (20 min): Comparison essay plan: "Compare distance-vector and link-state routing for a university campus network." Bullet points only.
  • Plenary (15 min): Class discussion of comparison points. Teacher models structured comparison paragraph.

Resources

  • Network topology worksheets (printed A3)
  • Dijkstra's algorithm step-by-step template
  • Cisco Packet Tracer simulation files (if available)
  • Video: "Distance Vector vs Link State Routing" (NetworkChuck)

Assessment Points

  • Summative: Quiz 2 (Week 6) — Networks & Routing (20 marks)
  • Formative: Dijkstra's algorithm tracing worksheet
  • Homework: Complete Dijkstra's on a given network; write 3 comparison points between distance-vector and link-state
Bronze: Identify routing table entries; follow provided Dijkstra's steps; name one limitation of distance-vector.
Silver: Calculate routing tables for small networks; complete Dijkstra's algorithm with partial guidance; explain count-to-infinity.
Gold: Solve complex network routing problems; trace Dijkstra's from scratch; evaluate routing protocol choice for given scenarios; explain split-horizon and poison reverse.

Week 7: DNS Deep Dive

Week 7: Domain Name System (DNS)

Learning Objectives

  • Explain the hierarchical structure of DNS (root, TLD, authoritative, subdomain)
  • Describe the DNS resolution process: recursive and iterative queries
  • Identify common DNS record types: A, AAAA, CNAME, MX, NS, TXT, SOA
  • Explain the role of caching in DNS performance
  • Discuss DNS security concerns and DNSSEC

Lesson Activities

Lesson 1 (2 hours):

  • Starter (10 min): "What happens when you type www.google.com?" Collect initial ideas.
  • Direct Instruction (30 min): DNS as the "phonebook of the Internet." Hierarchical tree structure: root servers (.), TLD servers (.com, .org, .uk), authoritative servers (example.com). Recursive resolver role.
  • Worked Example (20 min): Teacher traces complete DNS resolution for www.example.com. Show recursive query from client to resolver, then iterative queries from resolver to root, TLD, and authoritative servers.
  • Guided Practice (25 min): Students trace resolution for mail.vpa.edu.cn on provided diagram. Label each query and response. Pairs compare traces.
  • Plenary (5 min): Exit ticket: "Why is DNS hierarchical rather than flat?"

Lesson 2 (2 hours):

  • Starter (10 min): Review DNS hierarchy. "What information does each server actually store?"
  • Direct Instruction (25 min): DNS record types: A (IPv4 address), AAAA (IPv6), CNAME (alias), MX (mail server), NS (name server), TXT (text), SOA (start of authority). Show real dig/nslookup output.
  • Worked Example (15 min): Interpret a zone file snippet. Identify record types and their purposes.
  • Guided Practice (25 min): Students given 5 scenarios ("A company wants email at mail.example.com" — which record?"). Match scenarios to record types.
  • Independent Practice (25 min): Research task: "What is DNSSEC? How does it prevent DNS spoofing?" Students read provided article and summarise in 5 bullet points.
  • Plenary (10 min): Share DNSSEC summaries. Discuss: "Why isn't DNSSEC universally deployed?"

Resources

  • DNS hierarchy diagram (A1 poster)
  • Real dig command output samples
  • Article: "DNSSEC: What It Is and Why It Matters" (ICANN)
  • Online tool: https://dns.google/ for live queries

Assessment Points

  • Formative: DNS trace diagram (Lesson 1)
  • Formative: Record type matching exercise
  • Homework: Use nslookup/dig to query 3 domains; record and interpret A, MX, and NS records
Bronze: Name the levels of DNS hierarchy; identify A and MX records.
Silver: Trace complete DNS resolution; identify all common record types; explain caching benefits.
Gold: Analyse real DNS data; explain DNSSEC operation; evaluate security trade-offs; discuss CDN implications for DNS.

Week 8: Email Protocols & Web Technologies

Week 8: Email Protocols, HTML & CSS Basics

Learning Objectives

  • Compare SMTP, POP3, and IMAP protocols for email transmission and retrieval
  • Explain the role of MIME in supporting non-ASCII email content
  • Describe HTTP request methods (GET, POST, PUT, DELETE) and response codes
  • Construct basic HTML documents using semantic tags
  • Apply CSS selectors and properties to style HTML elements

Lesson Activities

Lesson 1 (2 hours):

  • Starter (10 min): "How many emails did you send yesterday? What happened behind the scenes?"
  • Direct Instruction (25 min): Email architecture: MUA (Mail User Agent) → MSA → MTA → MDA → MUA. SMTP for sending (port 25/587). POP3 (port 110) vs IMAP (port 143) for retrieval. Compare: POP3 downloads and deletes; IMAP syncs across devices.
  • Worked Example (15 min): Trace an email from Alice@example.com to Bob@sample.org. Show SMTP handshake, envelope vs header, MIME for attachments.
  • Guided Practice (25 min): Comparison table: SMTP vs POP3 vs IMAP across 6 criteria (direction, port, persistence, sync, offline access, security). Pairs complete, then class verifies.
  • Plenary (5 min): Quick-fire: "Which protocol would you use on a phone? On a shared library computer?"

Lesson 2 (2 hours):

  • Starter (10 min): "What happens when you click a link?" Review HTTP from Week 5.
  • Direct Instruction (20 min): HTTP methods: GET (retrieve), POST (submit), PUT (update), DELETE (remove). Status codes: 1xx informational, 2xx success, 3xx redirect, 4xx client error, 5xx server error. Common codes: 200, 301, 404, 500.
  • Worked Example (10 min): Teacher live-codes a basic HTML page: <!DOCTYPE html>, <html>, <head>, <body>, semantic tags (<header>, <nav>, <main>, <footer>).
  • Guided Practice (30 min): Students create a personal profile page in HTML. Bronze: basic structure with headings and paragraphs; Silver: add lists, links, and an image; Gold: add a table and form elements.
  • Independent Practice (30 min): Add CSS styling. Bronze: change colours and fonts; Silver: add class selectors, box model properties; Gold: responsive design with media queries, flexbox layout.
  • Plenary (10 min): Showcase 3 student pages. Peer feedback using rubric: structure, semantics, styling, creativity.

Resources

  • Email protocol comparison handout
  • HTTP status code reference card
  • CodePen.io accounts for HTML/CSS practice
  • MDN Web Docs: HTML and CSS references

Assessment Points

  • Formative: Protocol comparison table
  • Formative: HTML/CSS practical task marked against rubric
  • Homework: Create a 3-page website about a topic of choice; include navigation, styled with CSS
Bronze: Identify SMTP, POP3, IMAP purposes; create basic HTML page with headings and paragraphs.
Silver: Compare email protocols across criteria; create structured HTML with semantic tags and basic CSS styling.
Gold: Explain MIME and SMTP authentication; create responsive multi-page website with advanced CSS; explain HTTP caching and security headers.

Week 9: Consolidation & Formative Assessment

Week 9: Review, Practice & Formative Test

Learning Objectives

  • Consolidate understanding of Weeks 1–8 topics
  • Identify personal strengths and areas for improvement
  • Apply examination techniques to structured questions
  • Peer-teach challenging concepts to reinforce understanding

Lesson Activities

Lesson 1 (2 hours):

  • Starter (10 min): "One thing I understand well / One thing I need help with" — sticky note activity.
  • Direct Instruction (15 min): Teacher addresses top 3 areas of confusion from sticky notes (typically: K-map grouping, Dijkstra's algorithm, DNS resolution).
  • Guided Practice (45 min): Rotating revision stations: (1) Boolean algebra & K-maps, (2) Floating-point & normalisation, (3) TCP/IP & routing, (4) DNS & email. Students spend 10 minutes at each station with differentiated problem sets.
  • Peer Teaching (30 min): Students paired across ability levels. Each explains one concept to their partner. Teacher circulates with assessment checklist.
  • Plenary (10 min): "What will you revise tonight?" Personal revision plan written.

Lesson 2 (2 hours):

  • Formative Test (90 min): 40-mark paper covering Weeks 1–8. Mix of short answer, calculation, and extended response. Bilingual glossary provided.
  • Self-Assessment (15 min): Students mark their own work using provided mark scheme. Identify errors and misconceptions. Complete reflection sheet.
  • Target Setting (15 min): Students set 3 specific targets for improvement before mock exam. Teacher reviews and signs off.

Resources

  • Revision station problem sets (4 levels per station)
  • Formative test paper (40 marks, 90 minutes)
  • Mark scheme and reflection sheet
  • Personal revision plan template

Assessment Points

  • Formative: 40-mark test (Weeks 1–8) — diagnostic, not graded
  • Formative: Self-assessment and reflection sheet
  • Formative: Personal target-setting document

EAL Support — Week 9

  • Bilingual glossary for all technical terms used in test
  • Extra reading time (+10 minutes for 90-minute test)
  • Visual diagrams provided for all network questions
