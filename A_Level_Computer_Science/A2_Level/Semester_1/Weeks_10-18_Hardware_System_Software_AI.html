Week 10: Logic Circuits & Boolean Implementation

Week 10: Logic Circuits & Boolean Implementation

Learning Objectives

  • Design combinational logic circuits from Boolean expressions
  • Construct truth tables for multi-gate circuits
  • Implement circuits using NAND and NOR universal gates
  • Explain the relationship between Boolean algebra and physical hardware
  • Use Logisim to simulate and verify circuit designs

Lesson Activities

Lesson 1 (2 hours):

  • Starter (10 min): Recall Boolean laws from Week 1. "If I have the expression A·B + A·C, what circuit would I build?"
  • Direct Instruction (25 min): From expression to circuit: AND gates for products, OR gates for sums, NOT gates for complements. Show how to read a Boolean expression and draw the corresponding circuit diagram. Introduce circuit conventions: inputs on left, outputs on right, flow left-to-right.
  • Worked Example (20 min): Teacher designs circuit for F = A·B' + B·C. Step-by-step: identify AND terms, add NOT gates where needed, OR the results. Verify with truth table.
  • Guided Practice (25 min): Students design circuits for 3 expressions: Bronze (2-input), Silver (3-input with one NOT), Gold (4-input with multiple complements). Use Logisim on lab computers or paper templates.
  • Plenary (10 min): One student presents Gold solution. Class identifies any errors.

Lesson 2 (2 hours):

  • Starter (10 min): "Why do manufacturers prefer NAND gates over AND gates?" Discuss cost and universality.
  • Direct Instruction (25 min): Universal gates: NAND and NOR can implement any Boolean function. Demonstrate: NOT from NAND, AND from NAND, OR from NAND. Show De Morgan's equivalence: A·B = (A NAND B) NAND (A NAND B).
  • Worked Example (15 min): Convert F = A·B + C' to all-NAND implementation. Teacher draws original circuit, then replaces each gate with NAND equivalents.
  • Guided Practice (30 min): Students convert 3 circuits to all-NAND and all-NOR implementations. Verify using Logisim simulation.
  • Independent Practice (25 min): Design challenge: "Build a 2-to-1 multiplexer using only NAND gates." Students work individually or in pairs.
  • Plenary (10 min): Showcase multiplexer designs. Discuss why NAND is preferred in CMOS technology (fewer transistors).

Resources

  • Logisim installed on all lab computers
  • Circuit diagram templates (printed)
  • Physical logic gate kits for demonstration (if available)
  • Video: "Why NAND and NOR are Universal Gates" (Neso Academy)

Assessment Points

  • Formative: Circuit design worksheet (3 problems)
  • Formative: Logisim simulation files submitted
  • Homework: Design circuit for F = (A + B)·(C' + D) using only NAND gates; verify with truth table
Bronze: Draw circuits from simple 2-variable expressions; identify gate types.
Silver: Design multi-gate circuits from 3-variable expressions; convert to NAND/NOR implementations.
Gold: Design complex circuits from word problems; implement using only universal gates; explain CMOS advantages; design multiplexers.

Week 11: Half-Adders & Full-Adders

Week 11: Half-Adders & Full-Adders

Learning Objectives

  • Construct truth tables for half-adders and full-adders
  • Derive Boolean expressions for sum and carry outputs
  • Design half-adder and full-adder circuits using logic gates
  • Explain how full-adders are chained to create multi-bit adders
  • Calculate propagation delay in ripple-carry adders

Lesson Activities

Lesson 1 (2 hours):

  • Starter (10 min): Binary addition review: 101 + 110. What are the outputs at each bit position?
  • Direct Instruction (25 min): Half-adder: adds two 1-bit numbers, produces sum and carry. Truth table, Boolean expressions (Sum = A ⊕ B, Carry = A·B). Circuit diagram with XOR and AND gates.
  • Worked Example (15 min): Teacher builds half-adder in Logisim, tests all input combinations, verifies truth table.
  • Guided Practice (20 min): Students construct half-adder truth table and circuit from scratch. Bronze: complete provided diagram; Silver: derive from truth table; Gold: implement using only NAND gates.
  • Direct Instruction (15 min): Full-adder: adds three 1-bit numbers (A, B, Carry-in). Truth table with 8 rows. Derive Sum = A ⊕ B ⊕ Cin, Cout = A·B + (A ⊕ B)·Cin.
  • Plenary (5 min): Exit ticket: "Why can't a half-adder be used for all bit positions in multi-bit addition?"

Lesson 2 (2 hours):

  • Starter (10 min): Review full-adder expressions. "How do we add 4-bit numbers?"
  • Direct Instruction (20 min): Ripple-carry adder: chain full-adders. Carry-out of stage n becomes carry-in of stage n+1. 4-bit adder requires 4 full-adders. Show block diagram.
  • Worked Example (15 min): Trace 4-bit addition: 0110 + 0101. Show carry propagation through each stage. Calculate total propagation delay: 4 × gate delay.
  • Guided Practice (25 min): Students trace 2 additions on provided 4-bit adder diagram. Calculate propagation delay assuming 2ns per gate. Bronze: one addition; Silver: two additions with delay; Gold: identify worst-case delay path.
  • Independent Practice (30 min): Research and explain: "What is a carry-lookahead adder? How does it improve on ripple-carry?" Students prepare a 3-minute explanation with diagram.
  • Plenary (10 min): 2 students present carry-lookahead explanations. Class discussion: when is the extra complexity worth it?

Resources

  • Logisim adder circuits (pre-built for demonstration)
  • Printed full-adder truth table templates
  • Ripple-carry adder diagram (A3 poster)
  • Article: "Carry-Lookahead Adders" (University notes)

Assessment Points

  • Summative: Quiz 3 (Week 11) — Hardware & Adders (20 marks)
  • Formative: Ripple-carry tracing worksheet
  • Homework: Design a 4-bit ripple-carry adder in Logisim; test with 3 input pairs; calculate propagation delay
Bronze: Complete half-adder truth table; identify sum and carry expressions; follow provided ripple-carry diagram.
Silver: Derive full-adder expressions; design full-adder circuit; trace multi-bit addition with carry propagation.
Gold: Implement full-adder with universal gates; calculate and optimise propagation delay; research and explain carry-lookahead; evaluate speed vs complexity trade-offs.

Week 12: Flip-Flops & Sequential Logic

Week 12: Flip-Flops & Sequential Logic

Learning Objectives

  • Explain the difference between combinational and sequential logic
  • Describe the operation of SR, D, and JK flip-flops
  • Construct truth tables (characteristic tables) for each flip-flop type
  • Explain the role of clock signals in synchronous circuits
  • Design simple sequential circuits: counters and shift registers

Lesson Activities

Lesson 1 (2 hours):

  • Starter (10 min): "What's the difference between a light switch and a toggle switch?" Introduce concept of state.
  • Direct Instruction (25 min): Combinational vs sequential logic: combinational output depends only on current inputs; sequential output depends on current inputs AND previous state. Introduce flip-flop as 1-bit memory element. SR flip-flop: Set-Reset with NOR/NAND implementation. Characteristic table, forbidden state (S=R=1 for NOR, S=R=0 for NAND).
  • Worked Example (15 min): Teacher traces SR flip-flop behaviour for input sequence: S=1,R=0 → S=0,R=0 → S=0,R=1 → S=0,R=0. Show how state is maintained.
  • Guided Practice (25 min): Students complete SR flip-flop timing diagram for given input sequence. Bronze: complete provided diagram; Silver: draw from scratch; Gold: identify and explain the forbidden state condition.
  • Direct Instruction (15 min): D flip-flop: data input sampled on clock edge. Eliminates forbidden state. Positive-edge triggered vs negative-edge triggered. Show symbol with triangle (dynamic input).
  • Plenary (10 min): Compare SR and D flip-flops. "When would you use each?"

Lesson 2 (2 hours):

  • Starter (10 min): "How does a digital clock count seconds?" Introduce counters.
  • Direct Instruction (25 min): JK flip-flop: versatile with J=K=1 toggle mode. Characteristic table. T flip-flop (toggle) as special case. Binary counter: chain T flip-flops, each toggling when previous stage transitions from 1 to 0. Show 2-bit and 3-bit counter diagrams.
  • Worked Example (15 min): Teacher traces 3-bit binary counter through 8 clock cycles. Show output sequence: 000, 001, 010, 011, 100, 101, 110, 111, 000.
  • Guided Practice (25 min): Students design a 2-bit counter using D flip-flops. Derive next-state logic from state table. Bronze: complete provided design; Silver: design from state table; Gold: design modulo-5 counter (counts 0-4).
  • Independent Practice (25 min): Research: "What is a shift register? Name two applications." Students find real-world examples (serial communication, multiplication).
  • Plenary (10 min): Share shift register applications. Connect to USB, SATA, and other serial protocols.

Resources

  • Logisim flip-flop library (D, T, JK, SR)
  • Flip-flop timing diagram templates
  • Counter simulation files
  • Video: "Flip-Flops and Registers" (Neso Academy)

Assessment Points

  • Formative: Flip-flop timing diagram worksheet
  • Formative: Counter design task (marked with rubric)
  • Homework: Complete characteristic tables for SR, D, and JK flip-flops; draw timing diagram for given input sequence
Bronze: Complete provided timing diagrams; identify flip-flop types from symbols; follow counter sequences.
Silver: Draw timing diagrams from scratch; design simple counters; explain clock edge triggering; identify forbidden states.
Gold: Design non-standard counters (modulo-N); derive next-state logic from state tables; explain metastability; research and present on synchronous vs asynchronous counters.

Week 13: Microprocessors & Assembly Language

Week 13: Microprocessors & Assembly Language

Learning Objectives

  • Describe the main components of a CPU: ALU, CU, registers, buses
  • Explain the von Neumann architecture and stored program concept
  • Trace the fetch-decode-execute cycle step-by-step
  • Interpret and write simple assembly language instructions
  • Explain the role of the program counter, instruction register, and accumulator

Lesson Activities

Lesson 1 (2 hours):

  • Starter (10 min): "What happens inside your phone when you open an app?" Brainstorm CPU components.
  • Direct Instruction (30 min): Von Neumann architecture: CPU, memory, I/O connected by buses (address, data, control). CPU components: ALU (arithmetic/logic), CU (control unit), registers (PC, IR, ACC, MDR, MAR). Stored program concept: instructions and data in same memory.
  • Worked Example (20 min): Teacher traces fetch-decode-execute cycle for instruction LDA 5 (load accumulator with value at address 5). Show register contents at each step. Use diagram with arrows showing data flow.
  • Guided Practice (25 min): Students trace fetch-decode-execute for ADD 3 (add value at address 3 to accumulator). Bronze: follow provided template; Silver: trace independently; Gold: trace a 3-instruction sequence (LDA, ADD, STA).
  • Plenary (5 min): "Why is the PC incremented during the fetch phase?"

Lesson 2 (2 hours):

  • Starter (10 min): Review fetch-decode-execute. "How does the CPU know which operation to perform?"
  • Direct Instruction (25 min): Assembly language: mnemonic instructions corresponding to machine code. AQA-style instruction set: LDM, STO, ADD, SUB, JMP, CMP, JPE, JPN, OUT, END. Addressing modes: immediate (#) and direct. Demonstrate with simple program: add two numbers and output result.
  • Worked Example (15 min): Teacher writes and traces assembly program to calculate 15 + 7. Show memory contents, register values, and output at each step using AQA assembler simulator.
  • Guided Practice (30 min): Students write assembly programs: Bronze — load two values, add, store; Silver — calculate average of three numbers; Gold — implement a loop that counts from 1 to 10.
  • Independent Practice (25 min): Debug challenge: given a buggy assembly program, identify and fix errors. Program should multiply a number by 4 using repeated addition.
  • Plenary (10 min): Discuss: "Why do we still learn assembly when high-level languages exist?" (embedded systems, performance, security)

Resources

  • AQA Assembly Language Simulator (https://www.peterhigginson.co.uk/AQA/)
  • CPU architecture diagram (A1 poster)
  • Fetch-decode-execute tracing template
  • Assembly language instruction reference card

Assessment Points

  • Formative: Fetch-decode-execute tracing worksheet
  • Formative: Assembly program marking (functionality and style)
  • Homework: Write assembly program to find largest of three numbers; trace execution showing all register values
Bronze: Name CPU components; follow provided fetch-decode-execute traces; write simple 3-line assembly programs.
Silver: Trace fetch-decode-execute independently; write assembly programs with branching; explain register roles.
Gold: Write complex assembly with loops and conditionals; trace multi-instruction sequences; compare CISC vs RISC; explain pipelining concept.

Week 14: OS Memory Management

Week 14: Operating System Memory Management

Learning Objectives

  • Explain the purpose of memory management in operating systems
  • Describe paging: fixed-size pages, page tables, logical to physical address translation
  • Describe segmentation: variable-sized segments, segment tables
  • Compare paging and segmentation advantages and disadvantages
  • Explain virtual memory and the role of page faults

Lesson Activities

Lesson 1 (2 hours):

  • Starter (10 min): "Why can't we just load every program into RAM at once?" Discuss memory limitations.
  • Direct Instruction (25 min): Memory management goals: allocation, protection, sharing, virtualisation. Paging introduced: physical memory divided into frames, logical memory into pages. Page table maps page numbers to frame numbers. Address translation: split logical address into page number and offset.
  • Worked Example (20 min): Teacher demonstrates address translation. Page size = 1024 bytes. Logical address 2050. Page number = 2, offset = 2. Page table: page 2 → frame 5. Physical address = 5 × 1024 + 2 = 5122.
  • Guided Practice (25 min): Students complete 5 address translation exercises. Bronze: provided page table and simple addresses; Silver: construct page table from given mapping; Gold: calculate page table size and overhead.
  • Plenary (10 min): "What happens if a program tries to access a page not in memory?" Introduce page fault.

Lesson 2 (2 hours):

  • Starter (10 min): "Is dividing memory into fixed-size chunks always ideal?" Introduce segmentation.
  • Direct Instruction (25 min): Segmentation: logical program structure (code, data, stack, heap) mapped to variable-sized segments. Segment table contains base address and limit. Address translation: segment number + offset, with bounds checking.
  • Worked Example (15 min): Teacher demonstrates segmentation address translation with bounds checking. Show how segmentation prevents overflow (offset > limit → trap).
  • Guided Practice (25 min): Comparison table: paging vs segmentation. Students complete across 8 criteria (fragmentation, sharing, protection, table size, hardware support, etc.).
  • Independent Practice (30 min): Virtual memory research: "How does virtual memory allow a computer to run programs larger than physical RAM?" Students explain paging to disk (swap space), page fault handling, and thrashing.
  • Plenary (10 min): Class discussion: "Why do modern OSes use paging rather than pure segmentation?" (Linux uses paging; x86 segmentation is legacy)

Resources

  • Memory management diagram (A1 poster)
  • Address translation worksheets
  • Virtual memory animation (online)
  • Article: "Understanding the Linux Virtual Memory Manager"

Assessment Points

  • Summative: Quiz 4 (Week 14) — System Software (20 marks)
  • Formative: Address translation worksheet
  • Homework: Complete 8 address translations (4 paging, 4 segmentation); explain virtual memory with diagram
Bronze: Follow provided address translation steps; identify page number and offset; complete comparison table with guidance.
Silver: Perform address translation independently; construct page tables; explain internal vs external fragmentation; describe virtual memory operation.
Gold: Calculate page table overhead; analyse segmentation with multiple segments; explain page replacement algorithms (FIFO, LRU); discuss thrashing and working set model.

Week 15: Scheduling Algorithms

Week 15: CPU Scheduling Algorithms

Learning Objectives

  • Explain why CPU scheduling is necessary in multiprogramming systems
  • Describe and apply First-Come First-Served (FCFS) scheduling
  • Describe and apply Shortest Job First (SJF) scheduling
  • Describe and apply Round Robin (RR) scheduling with time quantum
  • Describe and apply Priority scheduling
  • Calculate waiting time, turnaround time, and response time for each algorithm

Lesson Activities

Lesson 1 (2 hours):

  • Starter (10 min): "If 5 students need to use one printer, how do you decide who goes first?" Analogies for scheduling.
  • Direct Instruction (25 min): Scheduling criteria: CPU utilisation, throughput, turnaround time, waiting time, response time. FCFS: simple queue, non-preemptive. SJF: minimises average waiting time, requires prediction. Preemptive vs non-preemptive.
  • Worked Example (20 min): Teacher calculates FCFS and SJF for 4 processes with given burst times. Show Gantt chart construction. Calculate average waiting time for each.
  • Guided Practice (25 min): Students complete Gantt charts and waiting time calculations for 3 scenarios. Bronze: FCFS only; Silver: FCFS and SJF; Gold: preemptive SJF (shortest remaining time first).
  • Plenary (10 min): "What are the disadvantages of SJF?" (starvation of long jobs, prediction difficulty)

Lesson 2 (2 hours):

  • Starter (10 min): "What if a new urgent job arrives while another is running?" Introduce preemption and Round Robin.
  • Direct Instruction (25 min): Round Robin: cyclic execution with fixed time quantum. Priority scheduling: highest priority runs first (preemptive or non-preemptive). Priority inversion problem. Multi-level feedback queues introduced briefly.
  • Worked Example (15 min): Teacher traces Round Robin with time quantum = 4 for 3 processes. Show context switches and partial execution.
  • Guided Practice (25 min): Students complete Round Robin and priority scheduling problems. Calculate turnaround time and response time. Bronze: Round Robin with 2 processes; Silver: Round Robin with 4 processes + priority; Gold: all algorithms with comparison of average metrics.
  • Independent Practice (25 min): Evaluation task: "A real-time system controls a medical device. Which scheduling algorithm is most appropriate? Justify your answer with criteria."
  • Plenary (10 min): Share evaluations. Discuss: no single algorithm is best for all scenarios.

Resources

  • Scheduling algorithm worksheets with Gantt chart templates
  • Online scheduler simulator
  • Comparison table template
  • Video: "CPU Scheduling Algorithms" (Neso Academy)

Assessment Points

  • Formative: Gantt chart and calculation worksheets
  • Formative: Evaluation task rubric assessment
  • Homework: Complete scheduling problems for all 4 algorithms; calculate average waiting time and turnaround time; recommend algorithm for given scenario
Bronze: Construct FCFS Gantt charts; calculate waiting time with guidance; identify basic scheduling criteria.
Silver: Apply all four algorithms; calculate waiting time, turnaround time, and response time; construct accurate Gantt charts.
Gold: Solve complex scheduling with arrival times and preemption; evaluate algorithms for specific scenarios; explain priority inversion and multi-level feedback queues; discuss real-time scheduling constraints.

Week 16: Interrupts & System Security

Week 16: Interrupts, Privilege Levels & System Security

Learning Objectives

  • Explain the purpose and types of interrupts (hardware, software, timer)
  • Describe the interrupt handling process: detection, saving state, executing handler, restoring state
  • Explain the role of the interrupt vector table
  • Describe privilege levels (user mode vs kernel mode) and protection rings
  • Explain how privilege levels protect system resources

Lesson Activities

Lesson 1 (2 hours):

  • Starter (10 min): "What happens when you press Ctrl+C while a program is running?" Introduce interrupts.
  • Direct Instruction (25 min): Interrupts: signals that pause normal execution. Types: hardware (I/O completion, keyboard), software (exceptions, system calls), timer (preemption). Interrupt handling: save PC and registers, load handler address, execute handler, restore state, return. Interrupt priority and masking.
  • Worked Example (15 min): Teacher traces keyboard interrupt: user presses key, keyboard controller sends signal, CPU saves state, handler reads keycode, stores in buffer, restores state, resumes program.
  • Guided Practice (25 min): Students trace a timer interrupt in a multitasking system. Show how timer interrupt enables process switching. Bronze: follow provided trace; Silver: identify all saved/restored registers; Gold: explain interrupt nesting and priority.
  • Plenary (5 min): "Why are interrupts essential for multitasking?"

Lesson 2 (2 hours):

  • Starter (10 min): "Why can't a normal program directly access the hard disk?" Introduce privilege levels.
  • Direct Instruction (25 min): Protection rings: Ring 0 (kernel/supervisor mode) to Ring 3 (user mode). Privileged instructions only in Ring 0. System calls as controlled gateway from user to kernel mode. Memory protection: base and limit registers, page table protection bits.
  • Worked Example (15 min): Teacher traces a system call: program requests file read → trap to kernel → kernel validates request → performs I/O → returns result to user program. Show mode switch on diagram.
  • Guided Practice (25 min): Scenario analysis: "A virus tries to modify the page table directly." What prevents this? Students identify protection mechanisms at each level.
  • Independent Practice (25 min): Research: "Meltdown and Spectre — how do hardware vulnerabilities bypass privilege levels?" Students summarise in 5 bullet points.
  • Plenary (10 min): Discuss Meltdown/Spectre. Implications for system security and hardware design.

Resources

  • Interrupt handling diagram (A1 poster)
  • Protection rings diagram
  • System call tracing worksheet
  • Article: "Meltdown and Spectre Explained" (Ars Technica)

Assessment Points

  • Formative: Interrupt tracing worksheet
  • Formative: Privilege level scenario analysis
  • Homework: Draw interrupt handling sequence diagram; explain 3 ways privilege levels protect system resources
Bronze: Name interrupt types; follow provided interrupt handling sequence; identify user vs kernel mode.
Silver: Trace interrupt handling independently; explain interrupt vector table; describe system call mechanism; explain memory protection.
Gold: Explain interrupt nesting and priority; analyse protection ring architecture; discuss hardware vulnerabilities (Meltdown/Spectre); evaluate security implications of privilege level design.

Week 17: Expert Systems & Neural Networks

Week 17: Expert Systems & Neural Networks

Learning Objectives

  • Describe the components of an expert system: knowledge base, inference engine, user interface
  • Explain forward chaining and backward chaining inference strategies
  • Evaluate the advantages and limitations of expert systems
  • Describe the structure and operation of a biological neuron
  • Explain the perceptron model and its learning rule
  • Describe multi-layer neural networks and the role of hidden layers

Lesson Activities

Lesson 1 (2 hours):

  • Starter (10 min): "Can a computer diagnose an illness?" Show medical diagnosis app screenshot.
  • Direct Instruction (25 min): Expert system components: knowledge base (facts and rules), inference engine (applies rules), user interface (explanation facility). Rule format: IF condition THEN conclusion. Forward chaining: data-driven, starts with facts, derives conclusions. Backward chaining: goal-driven, starts with hypothesis, seeks supporting facts.
  • Worked Example (15 min): Teacher demonstrates forward chaining on animal identification expert system. Given facts: "has feathers," "can fly" → conclude "bird." Then demonstrates backward chaining: "Is it a bird?" → check feathers, beak, etc.
  • Guided Practice (25 min): Students complete forward and backward chaining exercises on a simple expert system (diagnosing computer faults). Bronze: follow provided rule set; Silver: add one new rule; Gold: design a 5-rule expert system for a domain of choice.
  • Plenary (5 min): "What are the limitations of expert systems?" (knowledge acquisition bottleneck, brittleness, no common sense)

Lesson 2 (2 hours):

  • Starter (10 min): "How does your brain recognise a face?" Introduce neural networks.
  • Direct Instruction (25 min): Biological neuron: dendrites (inputs), soma (processing), axon (output), synapse (connection). Artificial neuron (perceptron): weighted inputs, summation, activation function (step, sigmoid, ReLU). Perceptron learning rule: adjust weights based on error.
  • Worked Example (20 min): Teacher traces perceptron for AND gate. Initialise weights, calculate output for each input, adjust weights if error, repeat until converged.
  • Guided Practice (25 min): Students trace perceptron learning for OR gate. Bronze: provided weight update table; Silver: calculate updates independently; Gold: explain why XOR cannot be solved by single perceptron (linear separability).
  • Independent Practice (20 min): Multi-layer networks: input layer, hidden layer(s), output layer. Backpropagation introduced conceptually. Students explore TensorFlow Playground (online) to visualise hidden layers solving XOR.
  • Plenary (10 min): Discussion: "What can neural networks do that expert systems cannot?" (learning from data, generalisation, handling noise)

Resources

  • Expert system rule sets (printed)
  • TensorFlow Playground (https://playground.tensorflow.org/)
  • Perceptron tracing worksheets
  • Video: "How Neural Networks Learn" (3Blue1Brown)

Assessment Points

  • Summative: Quiz 5 (Week 17) — AI & Ethics (20 marks)
  • Formative: Expert system chaining exercises
  • Homework: Complete perceptron learning trace for AND gate; explain why multi-layer networks are needed for XOR; research one real-world expert system
Bronze: Identify expert system components; follow provided chaining examples; describe perceptron structure.
Silver: Apply forward and backward chaining independently; trace perceptron learning; explain activation functions; describe multi-layer networks.
Gold: Design original expert system; derive perceptron learning rule mathematically; explain backpropagation conceptually; evaluate expert systems vs neural networks for given problems.

Week 18: Machine Learning, Big Data & Ethics

Week 18: Machine Learning, Big Data & AI Ethics

Learning Objectives

  • Distinguish between supervised, unsupervised, and reinforcement learning
  • Describe the characteristics of big data: volume, velocity, variety, veracity
  • Explain how machine learning algorithms process big data
  • Identify ethical issues in AI: bias, privacy, accountability, transparency
  • Evaluate the societal impact of AI and big data technologies

Lesson Activities

Lesson 1 (2 hours):

  • Starter (10 min): "How does Netflix know what you want to watch?" Discuss recommendation systems.
  • Direct Instruction (25 min): Machine learning types: supervised (labelled data, classification/regression), unsupervised (unlabelled, clustering/association), reinforcement (agent, environment, rewards). Big data: 5 Vs — Volume (scale), Velocity (speed), Variety (types), Veracity (quality), Value (usefulness). Examples: social media, IoT sensors, financial transactions.
  • Worked Example (15 min): Teacher classifies 5 scenarios into ML types: spam filter (supervised), customer segmentation (unsupervised), game playing AI (reinforcement).
  • Guided Practice (25 min): Students classify 10 real-world applications into ML types and identify which "V" of big data is most relevant. Bronze: ML type only; Silver: ML type + relevant Vs; Gold: explain why a particular algorithm might be chosen.
  • Plenary (5 min): "What makes big data 'big'? Is it just the size?"

Lesson 2 (2 hours):

  • Starter (10 min): "Should an AI be allowed to decide who gets a loan?" Introduce ethics.
  • Direct Instruction (20 min): Ethical issues: algorithmic bias (training data reflects historical bias), privacy (surveillance, data collection), accountability (who is responsible for AI decisions?), transparency (black box problem), job displacement, autonomous weapons. Framework for evaluation: utilitarian, deontological, virtue ethics.
  • Guided Practice (25 min): Case study analysis: "Facial recognition in public spaces." Students evaluate using ethical framework in groups. Bronze: identify one ethical issue; Silver: analyse from two perspectives; Gold: propose policy recommendation with justification.
  • Independent Practice (30 min): Students prepare a 2-minute position statement on one AI ethics topic of their choice. Use evidence from provided articles.
  • Plenary (15 min): Mini-debate: 3 students present positions, class questions. Teacher summarises key ethical principles.

Resources

  • Machine learning type classification cards
  • Big data case studies (Netflix, Amazon, healthcare)
  • AI ethics articles: "Weapons of Math Destruction" (O'Neil), EU AI Act summary
  • Video: "The Ethics of AI" (Royal Institution)

Assessment Points

  • Formative: ML classification worksheet
  • Formative: Ethics case study group analysis
  • Homework: Write 500-word evaluation: "Evaluate the ethical implications of using AI for university admissions."
Bronze: Identify ML types from descriptions; name one ethical issue; express a personal opinion on AI use.
Silver: Classify applications accurately; explain big data characteristics; analyse ethical issues using structured framework; consider multiple perspectives.
Gold: Evaluate ML algorithm choices for specific data characteristics; synthesise ethical arguments from multiple philosophical positions; propose evidence-based policy recommendations; discuss regulatory frameworks (EU AI Act, etc.).
